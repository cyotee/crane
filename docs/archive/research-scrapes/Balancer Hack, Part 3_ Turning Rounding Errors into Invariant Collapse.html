Balancer Hack, Part 3: Turning Rounding Errors into Invariant Collapse

How a precision bug, combined with low liquidity, becomes a mechanism for repeatedly degrading Balancer’s internal pricing until repayment itself becomes arbitrage.

Up until now, everything has been preparation.

Part 1 gave us the bullet: a rounding bug hidden in Balancer’s scaling math.

Part 2 cocked the gun: we drained the pool until that bug had room to matter.

Now we pull the trigger.

This is the stage of the exploit in which the attacker begins forcing Balancer to undervalue its own pool, thereby crushing the invariant and dragging down the price of BPT with it.

Once that starts happening, the debt we owe the Vault becomes cheaper and cheaper to repay — while the tokens we already hold stay in our pocket.

This is the exact sequence of swaps that turns a harmless-looking rounding mismatch into a protocol-breaking machine.

Recap

In Part 1, we discovered the rounding bug and came up with a mission path to exploit it:

  1. Use batchSwap() to borrow BPT. Swap BPT tokens for the underlying pool tokens, driving the pool to low liquidity. We now have tokens and owe the Vault BPT.

  2. Exploit the rounding at low liquidity. Perform a _swapGivenOut() swap that triggers the rounding error in _upscale(), corrupting the invariant downward.

  3. Repay the BPT debt — at the new, lower rate. Because the invariant has fallen, we need fewer underlying tokens to pay back the Balancer Pool Token (BPT) we owe.

  4. Pocket the difference. We exit the batch having spent fewer tokens than we received.

In Part 2, we applied the first step of Part 1. We prepared a good environment for our bug to thrive by depleting the pool. After depleting the pool, we ended up with this state:

We successfully reduced the pool’s wETH and osETH balances, and shrank the Balancer Pool Token (BPT) supply (bptActualSupply). In return, we now hold a mountain of wETH and osETH — but we also owe the Vault 11847572643042372078679 BPT.

That debt is the constraint on the entire transaction.

If we repay it at the current BPT price, everything we have gained disappears. So the attacker’s next objective is not escape, but repricing: forcing BPT to become cheaper before repayment.

And that begins with one thing — finding the exact pair of numbers that makes the two relevant internal subtraction paths disagree (_processGeneralPoolSwapRequest() and _calcInGivenOut()).

Hunting the Numbers That Break Balancer

There are two hunts in this section.

I came up with the first one, but I suspect the second is closer to how the hacker actually hunted for the trigger values. In the second hack, we’ll end up at an inequality similar to the one BlockSec referenced in their post-mortem.

If you want to get to invariant reduction quickly, skip to the Hacker’s Hunt. If you want to stay with me for the full chase, read both.

Our Hunt

In Part 1, we established that to exploit the pool, we need two values that ensure:

Δ _processGeneralPoolSwapRequest()≠Δ _calcInGivenOut()

To find values that satisfy this condition, we can run a script that takes two values, executes the subtractions in these two functions and compares the results. If the outputs are different, the two values satisfy the condition.

The obvious way to find such values is brute force: write a script, run both subtractions against random inputs, and log the pairs where the outputs disagree.

But before we unleash a script blindly, we can shrink the search space.

Let’s look at the subtraction part of the two functions again.

_processGeneralPoolSwapRequest() subtraction is on line 439.

_calcInGivenOut() subtraction is on line 185.

As shown above, the larger value is the pool balance before the swap, and the smaller value is the amount being sent out.

So our condition translates to:

_upscale(balance)−_upscale(amountOut) ≠_upscale(balance−amountOut)

The left-hand side is a difference of two floors, while the right-hand side is a floor of a difference.

That distinction matters.

These two expressions obey two properties we can exploit:

  1. _upscale(y) - _upscale(x) >= _upscale(y-x)

  2. (_upscale(y) - _upscale(x)) - _upscale(y-x) <= 1

If you want the full proof of these floor properties, here’s a separate derivation.

The first tells us the left-hand side can be larger than the right-hand side.

The second tells us the gap between them can never exceed one.

That means we are not searching an infinite wilderness anymore — we are searching for a very thin crack. The disagreement can only be one unit wide, so the most efficient exploit must happen at consecutive numbers.

That narrows things dramatically.

We already know that two consecutive numbers will give us 1 from _upscale(y-x). What we do not yet know is whether those same two numbers can give us 2 from _upscale(y) - _upscale(x).

So now the brute-force script becomes useful.

Let’s run it.

In the script above, whenever a != b, a = 1 and b = 2. The scaling factor used is the scaling factor of the pool we are attacking.

Here are some values the script logged in [balance, amountOut] format:

[18,17], [35,34], [69,68], [121,120], [138,137], [155,154]

There it is.

A handful of narrow trigger windows where Balancer’s two subtraction paths stop agreeing.

Did you notice that we used the first set — [18,17] — back in Part 1 to test whether _calcInGivenOut() and _processGeneralPoolSwapRequest() are equivalent?

The attacker uses that same set to hack the wETH/osETH pool.

Because we want to follow the hacker’s footsteps, we’ll use it too. But the other sets would also work.

Still, I doubt the attacker arrived at [18,17] the same way we just did.

Let’s look at the hunt they likely did.

Hacker’s Hunt (Allegedly)

If you read Our Hunt, temporarily forget the brute force and come at this fresh.

Let’s start from the expression itself.

_upscale(y)−_upscale(x) ≠_upscale(y−x)

How do we get 2 from the left and 1 from the right?

Pause there for a second.

To get 1 on the right, we can simply choose any two consecutive numbers y and x where y > x.

But to get 2 on the left, something more specific has to happen:

_upscale(y) must jump by one, while _upscale(x) must not.

In other words:

x=_upscale(x,scalingFactor) and y+1=_upscale(y,scalingFactor)

So the hunt becomes more surgical.

We are no longer scanning blindly — we are looking for the exact point where flooring begins to bite one value but not the other.

Let’s first solve for x in _upscale(x) = x.

c is the decimal portion removed by flooring. Since c < 1, the equation becomes an inequality:

x<1sF−1

Substituting the scaling factor of the wETH/osETH pool (sF = 1.058132398695929516) gives:

x < 17.202111429

Since x must be a positive integer, the valid range is 1–17.

Any value in that range passes through _upscale() unchanged.

Now we do the same thing for y in _upscale(y) = y + 1.

Following the same steps, we arrive at:

y<2sF−1

Substituting the same scaling factor gives:

y < 34.4042228579

And since values up to 17 already belong to the first bucket, this second inequality is valid for the range 18–34.

Now the picture becomes clear.

We need two consecutive numbers:

  • one from the first range,

  • one from the second.

There is only one boundary where those two worlds touch:

17 and 18.

That is our trigger.

Take 18 as the balance.
Take 17 as the amount to swap out.

We found the exact seam where Balancer’s math begins to split.

Now let’s pull it.

Turning One Trigger into Thirty

One Successful Corruption

In part 2, we completed the depletion swaps, and our current balance of wETH and osETH is 51268. But we need the osETH balance to be 18 before we reduce the invariant.

This means we’ll need to swap to reduce the osETH balance to 18 and swap again to reduce the invariant. Let’s do that and see if it’ll reduce the invariant.

The first swap is a positioning swap.

Its only job is to drag the osETH balance down until the pool sits exactly within our trigger window. Since TRICK_AMT = 17, we swap out balances[2] - (TRICK_AMT + 1), leaving precisely 18 osETH behind.

That number matters.

We are deliberately steering Balancer into the narrow balance range where its two subtraction paths begin to disagree.

Before doing the second swap, we simulate the effect of this swap with _quoteAndApplyExploitSwap(), which updates our local balances array using a quoted input amount plus fees.

To get that quote, _quoteAndApplyExploitSwap() calls QUOTER.quoteAmountInWithFee() — a helper contract that will soon become far more important than it looks.

quoteAmountInWithFee() calls StableMath._calcInGivenOut() to get the wETH we’ll need to send in for the swap and adds the fee in line 49. The new wETH balance is all we need to update the balances array. Let’s go to the second swap.

The second swap is the main exploit swap.

Now that the pool has been pinned to 18 osETH, we ask Balancer to send out 17 — the exact amount that forces the rounding mismatch we hunted for earlier.

Again, we simulate the changes in the balance after the swap.

If our reasoning is correct, this is the point where Balancer’s accounting should finally split: _calcInGivenOut() will charge the pool as if more value remains than _processGeneralPoolSwapRequest() actually records.

And if that happens, the invariant falls.

Time to test it.

After the two swaps, Balancer’s internal accounting finally gives way.

We have proven the exploit works once.

Unfortunately, making it work repeatedly turned out to be a different battle.

Unfortunately, one successful corruption is not enough.

Remember that we passed 30 to the BalancerHacker constructor as the number of times we want to reduce the invariant. We’d need a loop to repeat this exploit, but first of all, we’ll need to return the osETH balance to 18. Currently, the osETH balance is 1, so let’s swap 17 osETH into the pool. But there’s an issue here. Can you figure it out? (Hint: It has to do with the swap direction in our call to batchSwap() in part 2).

The problem reveals itself in the swap direction. Our call to batchSwap() in the internal _exploit() function is below. It’s saying all our swaps should be of type SwapKind.GIVEN_OUT. This means that our swaps can only specify the amount to be sent out, not the amount to send in.

But we need to send in 18 osETH to reduce the invariant again. It’s easy to figure out this value off-chain, but it’ll take extra steps to do it on-chain. So what we’ll do is swap in an arbitrary amount of osETH and then swap out the excess, leaving 18 osETH in the pool. That’s two extra swaps.

Did you notice that the last swap I mentioned does the same thing as the first swap we did? Because of this, there’s no need for a fourth swap; we’ll bring the first swap into the loop and let it reduce the osETH balance to 18 on each iteration. Let’s write the third swap.

Balancer fights back

We’ll swap out half of the wETH balance in line 485 above to increase the osETH balance. But when I run it, I get an error.

The transaction reverted with BAL#001, which is Balancer’s swap overflow error. By the time execution returned to the first swap of the next iteration, the osETH balance was still below the minimum 18 units we needed. We rebuilt the pool — just not enough.

Looking at the original attacker’s transaction, we notice that he did not use arbitrary values here. Instead, he rounded the wETH amount down to two significant figures before attempting the last swap. Let’s mirror that approach and try again.

We updated the amount in line 485 to have two significant figures of the wETH balance. But when we run it, we get another revert.

This time, it didn’t go past the third swap. It reverted with the QUOTE_DIDNT_CONVERGE error.

That error comes from the Newton-Raphson iteration inside StableMath: Balancer repeatedly tries to solve for a valid new osETH balance, and for this particular candidate amount, the iterative search simply gives up without finding one.

This was the frustrating part.

Two candidate values could be numerically close to each other, yet one would pass through Newton-Raphson while the other would make the entire transaction collapse. There is no clean, closed-form answer that tells us which values are “safe.” We have to probe Balancer’s math experimentally.

And this is exactly where the attacker became clever.

Whenever the rounded candidate failed, he simply retried with 90% of that amount. If that failed too, he reduced again, gradually walking downward until Balancer accepted the quote, and the resulting osETH amount was large enough for the next exploit round.

In other words, our third swap can’t use a fixed number.

It has to search.

That search introduces a Solidity problem: every failed quote will revert the transaction, and one revert will wipe out the entire exploit batch.

The only practical way around this is try...catch.

But try...catch only works on external function calls, which means this probing logic will not live inside our main hacker contract. We have to move the quote attempt into a helper contract we’ll call QUOTER.

QUOTER.quoteAmountInWithFee() will become our onchain test environment. We pass it a candidate wETH amount, ask Balancer’s StableMath library how much osETH that will require, and if it rejects the candidate, try...catch absorbs the revert, and we immediately retry with a smaller amount.

Once a candidate finally succeeds, we still perform one last check: does this quote rebuild the osETH balance to at least 18?

If yes, we keep it.

If no, we use the observed exchange rate from that successful quote to estimate a better candidate and try again.

Only after this search finishes do we obtain a wETH amount that Balancer is willing to process — and that leaves the pool in a valid state for the next invariant-reduction cycle.

What looked like a simple refill swap had quietly turned into the most temperamental part of the entire exploit.

With the recycle swap finally stabilised, we put all three swaps inside a loop and let the exploit run repeatedly.

Balancer Gives Way

After running all the swaps, we reduced the invariant from 105515 to 2433. That’s a 97.69% decrease.

The invariant didn’t break by accident. It broke because we learned how to make it fall.

This is the crux of the entire Balancer hack. Everything that follows — including debt repayment and final profit extraction — depends on this repeated invariant corruption.

I have to give the attacker credit here: the third swap was maddening.
Values that looked almost identical could produce completely different Newton-Raphson outcomes, yet they kept probing until they found a stable recycle path.

Don’t forget that we still owe the protocol some money in the form of BPT. We have to pay back, or our transaction will revert. In the next part, we’ll pay back the BPT we owe, and I’ll show you something I discovered. I’ll see you there next week.
